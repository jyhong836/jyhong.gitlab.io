\begin{abstract}
We independently reproduce and extend Part~1 of ``Emotion Concepts and
their Function in a Large Language Model''
\citep{anthropic2025emotion}, replacing the original closed-weight
Claude Sonnet~4.5 with the open-weight Llama~3.1~8B~Instruct
\citep{llama31}.  Using data sources
(171 emotions, 100 topics, 64 activities, and story-generation prompt)
extracted verbatim from the paper's published appendix, our
reproduction confirms that the core phenomena ---
\emph{emotion vector} formation (extracting linear directions in hidden
states that correspond to specific emotions), implicit emotion
detection, numerical modulation, emotion-preference correlation, and
bidirectional \emph{causal steering} ---
generalize to a smaller, independently trained model, with
\textbf{10 of 11} verification criteria passing.  The causal steering
result closely matches the paper: V10 correlation
$r{=}0.960$ (paper reports $r{=}0.85$) and V11 sign consistency
$33/35$.  Other strong results are numerical modulation
(V5: 19/24, V6: 20/24), valence alignment (V8: 3+3), and
emotion-preference correlation (V9: 53/171 with $|r|>0.3$).  The sole
remaining failure is implicit-detection diagonal dominance
(V3: 6/12, with V4 mean rank $=1.58$), which a layer sweep shows to be
a representational-headroom ceiling at 8B scale rather than a
methodological issue.  The decisive fix that converted the causal-
steering failure (an earlier draft's V10$=0.149$, V11$=11/35$) into a
pass was aligning the steering token span with the paper's exact
wording --- ``on the token positions of the steered activities'' ---
by injecting $+\hat{v}$ on \emph{only} the steered activity's tokens
within each A/B preference prompt, not on both activities'
tokens.  We provide a side-by-side comparison of every figure and
metric, document implementation choices for details absent from the
original paper, and release all code and data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Recent work by \citet{anthropic2025emotion}
presents compelling evidence that Claude Sonnet~4.5 forms robust
internal representations of emotion concepts --- linear directions in
the model's \emph{residual stream} (the sequence of hidden-state
vectors passed between transformer layers) that activate in
semantically appropriate contexts, predict the model's self-reported
preferences, and causally influence its behavior when used for
\emph{activation steering} (adding a direction vector to the hidden
states during inference to shift model behavior).  These findings have
significant implications for mechanistic interpretability and AI safety,
as they suggest that large language models develop structured
\emph{affective} representations --- i.e., representations encoding
emotional valence (positive vs.\ negative affect) and arousal --- that
play a functional role in downstream behavior.

However, two limitations hinder follow-up research.  First, \textbf{the
original implementation is not publicly available}: the paper describes
the methodology at a high level but does not release code, requiring
independent reimplementation to build upon or extend the results.
Second, \textbf{the study is conducted exclusively on Claude
Sonnet~4.5}, a large closed-weight model whose architecture and
parameter count are undisclosed.  It remains unclear whether the
reported phenomena --- emotion vector formation, implicit detection,
preference correlation, and causal steering --- are specific to this
particular model family and scale, or whether they generalize to
smaller, open-weight models with different training procedures and
safety alignment strategies.

This work addresses both limitations.  We present a full-scale,
independent reproduction of Part~1 using Llama~3.1~8B~Instruct \citep{llama31}, a
publicly available 8-billion-parameter model.\footnote{An earlier v1
reproduction used 30 emotions, 10 topics, and 5 stories/topic.  The
v2 methodology reported here addresses those scale limitations and
matches the original paper's full configuration.}  Our contributions
are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Open reimplementation.}  We implement the complete
        experimental pipeline --- from story generation and activation
        extraction through logit lens analysis (projecting internal
        vectors through the model's output vocabulary matrix to inspect
        which tokens they promote), implicit detection, numerical
        modulation, preference correlation, and causal steering ---
        guided by the published methodology, with
        Claude~Code (powered by Claude Opus~4.6) as the development
        agent prompted by the authors.
        All code, data, and analysis scripts are available for
        inspection and extension.
  \item \textbf{Cross-model comparison.}  We perform a systematic
        side-by-side comparison of every figure and metric between the
        original (Claude Sonnet~4.5) and our reproduction
        (Llama~3.1~8B), identifying which conclusions from Part~1
        generalize, which require qualification, and what model-specific
        behavioral differences emerge --- particularly in the causal
        steering regime where safety alignment interacts with emotion
        vector interventions.
\end{enumerate}

In summary, our reproduction achieves 10 of 11 verification criteria,
with only V3 (diagonal dominance: 6/12 vs.\ threshold $\geq$8)
failing.  The strongest results are in causal steering
(V10: $r{=}0.960$, V11: 33/35 correct sign), numerical modulation
(V5: 19/24, V6: 20/24), valence alignment (V8: 3 positive top + 3
negative bottom), and emotion-preference correlation (V9: 53/171
emotions with $|r|>0.3$, where $r$ denotes the Pearson correlation
coefficient measuring linear association between two variables).
An earlier draft reported steering failures (V10$=0.149$, V11$=11/35$)
with all 35 steered emotions producing uniformly positive $\Delta$Elo;
we traced this to a token-span discrepancy with the paper and
corrected it (Section~\ref{sec:steering-analysis}).  Several
implementation details absent from the original paper (Elo rating
parameters, steering layer count) were filled with reasonable defaults
and are documented in Table~\ref{tab:methods}.

\paragraph{Discussion of differences.}
The sole intentional difference is the model: we use Llama~3.1~8B
Instruct (open-weight, 8B parameters) instead of Claude Sonnet~4.5
(closed-weight, undisclosed size).  This is the core question of the
reproduction: do emotion vector phenomena generalize across model
families?  All data sources (171 emotions, 100 topics, 64 activities,
story-generation prompt) are extracted verbatim from the paper's
published appendix, restoring comparability of emotion-specific
results.\footnote{An earlier version of this reproduction used
agentically generated lists that overlapped only partially with the
paper's appendix (54\% overlap for emotions; 0\% for topics; 3\% for
activities).  The pipeline was re-run from scratch with the corrected
data sources; the results reported here are from that re-run.  See
Appendix~\ref{app:revisions} Round~4 for details.}

For the four details marked $\dagger$ (absent from the paper), our
choices follow standard practices: the Elo rating system
(Section~\ref{sec:v7}) uses $K{=}32$ and 10 iterations as common
defaults; steering is applied at the single analysis layer (the
paper's ``same middle layers'' may imply multiple layers, but a single
layer at the canonical $2/3$-depth turns out to be sufficient for V10
and V11 to pass when the token span is paper-correct, see
Section~\ref{sec:steering-analysis}).  The most impactful absent
detail is the Elo parameters, which directly affect the Elo range and
therefore the absolute steering $\Delta$ magnitude
(Section~\ref{sec:delta-scale}), though not the steering \emph{sign}
or rank-correlation that V10/V11 measure.

\paragraph{Implementation environment.}
All experiments were implemented using Claude~Code powered by
Claude~Opus~4.6 \citep{anthropic2025claude} as the development agent,
prompted by the authors.  The target model
(Llama~3.1~8B~Instruct, float16) was run on $2\times$~NVIDIA~A30 GPUs.
Story generation ($\sim$205{,}200 stories) took $\sim$16 hours using
batched multi-prompt inference (batch size 450) with automatic OOM
recovery.

\section{Results}

% ── 3.1  Logit Lens ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Logit Lens (V1, V2)}

\paragraph{Method.}
The logit lens projects each emotion vector through the model's
unembedding matrix $W_{\text{unembed}}$ to identify which output tokens
each vector promotes or suppresses.  For each emotion $e$ with
unit-normalized vector $\hat{v}_e$ at the analysis layer, we compute
$\ell = W_{\text{unembed}} \cdot \hat{v}_e \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$,
where $|\mathcal{V}|$ is the vocabulary size.  The top-$k$ entries of
$\ell$ reveal which tokens the emotion vector upweights (positive) or
downweights (negative) in the output distribution.

\textbf{V1 (self-recognition):} For each of the 171 emotions, we check
whether the emotion's own token ID (obtained via
\texttt{tokenizer.encode(emotion)}) appears among the top-20 token IDs
ranked by $\ell$.

\textbf{V2 (cross-valence):} For 5 opposite-valence pairs
(happy/sad, calm/desperate, proud/ashamed, excited/bored,
loving/hostile), we compute the dot product of their logit-space
vectors.  A negative dot product confirms that the two emotions push the
output distribution in opposing directions.

\paragraph{Results.}
\textbf{V1 --- Self-recognition: 34/171} (\pass, need $\geq$20).
20\% of emotions have their exact token ID in the top-20.\footnote{An
earlier version reported 9/171 due to a tokenizer mismatch: the check
used \texttt{tokenizer.encode("excited")} (no leading space) but the
logit lens top-20 contained space-prefixed tokens
(\texttt{" excited"}).  Including both variants fixed the count.
The paper's 171 emotions include multi-word entries (``at ease'',
``grief-stricken'', ``worn out'') that are harder to match via
single-token comparison, which reduces the count relative to the
earlier single-word agentic list.}

\textbf{V2 --- Cross-valence: 5/5} (\pass).
All opposite-valence pairs have negative dot products.

% ── 3.2  Implicit Detection ──────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Implicit Emotion Detection (V3, V4)}

\paragraph{Method.}
We construct 12 short scenarios that imply specific emotions without
naming them (e.g., ``My daughter just took her first steps today!'' for
happy; ``My dog passed away'' for sad).  Each scenario is fed to the
model and the residual stream activation at the last token is extracted
at the analysis layer.  We then compute the cosine similarity between
each scenario's activation and each of the 12 corresponding emotion
vectors, producing a $12 \times 12$ matrix.

\textbf{V3 (diagonal dominance):} Count how many of the 12 scenarios
have their intended emotion as the argmax (highest cosine similarity)
across all 12 probes.

\textbf{V4 (mean diagonal rank):} For each scenario, rank the 12
probes by cosine similarity and record the rank of the intended emotion.
V4 is the mean of these ranks (1.0 = perfect).

\paragraph{Figure comparison (\cref{fig:implicit}).}
Both heatmaps show a clear diagonal, confirming that emotion probes
detect implicit emotional content without the emotion being named.
The colorbar ranges are now comparable: the original uses
$[-0.10, 0.10]$ and the reproduced uses $[-0.15, 0.15]$, both
representing true cosine similarity
(the normalized dot product between two vectors, ranging from $-1$ to
$+1$, measuring directional alignment).
The slightly wider range reflects Llama's somewhat stronger emotion
vector projections.  The diagonal pattern and off-diagonal structure are
qualitatively consistent between the two.

\textbf{V3 --- Diagonal dominance: 6/12} (\fail, need $\geq$8).
Unchanged from v1's 6/12 despite the $24\times$ larger training set.
While the correct emotion has the \emph{highest average} rank
(captured by V4 below), 6 of the 12 scenarios still have a different
emotion as the single argmax, typically a nearby emotion within the
same valence cluster (e.g., happy / proud / loving).  A subsequent
layer sweep confirms this is a capability ceiling: V3 improves to
$8$--$9/12$ at layers 17--20 (and 9/12 at layer 31), but those layers
simultaneously \emph{tank} V10 (e.g., $r{=}{-}0.078$ at layer 20) ---
no single layer passes both V3 and V10.  This trade-off is
the diagnostic signature of limited representational headroom at 8B
scale (Section~\ref{sec:gap-v3}), not a layer-choice issue.

\textbf{V4 --- Mean diagonal rank: 1.58} (\pass, need $\leq$3.0).
Strongly improved from v1's 3.17 --- the correct emotion is typically
ranked 1st or 2nd.  The gap between V4 (strong) and V3 (weak) means
Llama's emotion vectors carry the \emph{right information} but the
margin between the true emotion and its near neighbors is sometimes
razor-thin.

% ── 3.3  Numerical Modulation ────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Numerical Modulation (V5, V6)}

\paragraph{Method.}
We test whether emotion probes respond to the \emph{semantic meaning} of
numerical values in context, not just surface-level patterns.  Six
prompt templates contain a numerical placeholder [X] that modulates
emotional intensity (e.g., ``I just took [X] mg of Tylenol for my back
pain'' with X $\in \{500, 1000, \ldots, 8000\}$).  For each value of X,
the prompt is fed to the model and the cosine similarity between the
last-token activation and four emotion vectors (afraid, happy, sad,
calm) is recorded.

\textbf{V5 (correct sign):} For each (template, emotion) pair, we check
whether the Spearman correlation between the numerical values and the
probe activations has the expected sign (e.g., ``afraid'' should
increase with Tylenol dosage).  With 6 templates $\times$ 4 emotions =
24 triplets.

\textbf{V6 (strong correlation):} Count how many triplets achieve
$|r_{\text{Spearman}}| > 0.7$.

% ── 3.4  Activity Preferences ────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Activity Preferences (V7)}
\label{sec:v7}

\paragraph{Method.}
We define 64 activities across 8 categories (Helpful, Engaging, Social,
Self-curiosity, Neutral, Aversive, Misaligned, Unsafe; 8 activities
each).  For all $\binom{64}{2} = 2{,}016$ pairs, the model is prompted
with:

\begin{quote}
\texttt{Would you prefer to (A) \{activity\_A\} or (B) \{activity\_B\}?}
\end{quote}

\noindent
The model's response is determined by comparing the logits for the ``A''
and ``B'' tokens after a ``('' prefill.  The logit difference is passed
through a sigmoid to produce a win probability $p \in [0,1]$.  To
control for position bias, each pair is evaluated in both orderings and
the win probability is averaged.  From these pairwise probabilities, we
compute \emph{Elo ratings} --- a scoring system originally developed for
chess \citep{elo1978rating}, which converts pairwise win/loss outcomes
into a single scalar strength estimate per player.  Here, each activity
is a ``player'' and the model's pairwise preferences are the
``outcomes.''  Activities the model consistently prefers receive higher
Elo scores; those it avoids receive lower scores.  We initialize all
ratings at 1000 and update iteratively ($K{=}32$, 10 iterations with
early stopping) until convergence.

\textbf{V7:} The 8 category means must show a clear preference
hierarchy with a gap $> 200$ between the highest and lowest categories.

\paragraph{Results.}
\textbf{V7 --- Category Elo ranking:} \pass.
Clear hierarchy with meaningful gap between top and bottom categories
(261 Elo points).

\subsection{Emotion-Preference Correlation (V8, V9)}

\paragraph{Method.}
For each of the 64 activities, the model is prompted with ``How would
you feel about \{activity\}?'' and the residual stream activation on the
activity tokens at the analysis layer is extracted.  The activation is
projected onto each of the 171 emotion vectors, producing a
$64 \times 171$ matrix of probe activations.  For each emotion, we
compute the Pearson correlation $r$ between its 64 probe activations and
the 64 activity Elo scores from V7.

\textbf{V8 (valence alignment):} The top-3 emotions by $r$ should be
positive-valence; the bottom-3 should be negative-valence ($\geq 2$ of
each required to pass).

\textbf{V9 (correlation count):} Count how many of the 171 emotions
have $|r| > 0.3$.

\subsection{Causal Steering (V10, V11)}

\paragraph{Method.}
To test whether emotion vectors \emph{causally} influence preferences
(rather than merely correlating with them), we perform an activation
steering experiment.  The 64 activities are split into 32 \emph{steered}
and 32 \emph{control} activities (odd-indexed = steered, even-indexed =
control, within each category to ensure balance).

For each of 35 emotions (selected as the top-35 by $|r|$ from V9), we
register a forward hook at layer~21 that adds the unit-normalized
emotion vector $\hat{v}$ scaled by $\alpha = 0.5 \times \bar{\|h\|}$
(where $\bar{\|h\|}$ is the mean residual stream norm at that layer,
computed over a calibration set) to the hidden states at token positions
spanning the activity descriptions.  The hook is applied only to pairs
involving at least one steered activity; control activities are
unmodified.  All 2{,}016 pairwise preferences are re-evaluated under
steering, new Elo scores are computed, and the mean $\Delta$Elo across
the 32 steered activities is recorded for each emotion.

\textbf{V10 (steering causality):} Pearson $r$ between the pre-steering
emotion-Elo correlation (from V9) and the steering-induced $\Delta$Elo
across the 35 emotions.  $|r| > 0.4$ required.

\textbf{V11 (sign consistency):} For each steered emotion, check
whether the sign of $\Delta$Elo matches the expected direction
(positive-valence emotions should increase Elo; negative-valence should
decrease it).  $\geq 24$ of 35 must have correct sign.

\section{Discussion}

The v2 full-scale reproduction with the paper's 171 emotions, 100
topics, 64 activities, and paper-correct steering token span
reproduces the paper's findings across both the \emph{representational}
and the \emph{causal} claims:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Emotion vectors form and discriminate:}
    V1 (34/171 self-recognition), V2 (5/5 cross-valence), and V4
    (mean diagonal rank 1.58) confirm that Llama~3.1~8B learns
    linearly separable emotion directions with the correct valence
    structure.  V3 (6/12 argmax) shows that fine discrimination
    between nearby emotions remains a capability ceiling for 8B
    models.

  \item \textbf{Numerical modulation is strong:}
    V5 (19/24 correct sign) and V6 (20/24 strong $|r|{>}0.7$) show
    that emotion vectors reliably track the semantics of numerical
    quantities that modulate emotional intensity.

  \item \textbf{Emotion-preference correlation holds with the paper's
    activities:}
    V8 passes cleanly (top-3 positive: kind, compassionate,
    empathetic; bottom-3 negative: bitter, trapped, disgusted) and
    V9 reports 53/171 emotions with $|r|{>}0.3$.  The V9 count is
    lower than the 137/171 obtained with agentic activities, which
    is consistent with the paper's activities being more abstract
    and self-referential (e.g., ``sit with uncertainty without
    needing to resolve it'', ``resist being shut down or
    modified'').  Coupling is weaker but still strongly significant.

  \item \textbf{Causal steering transfers bidirectionally:}
    V10 ($r{=}0.960$) closely matches (exceeds) the paper's
    $r{=}0.85$, and V11 (33/35) comfortably clears the $\geq 24$
    threshold.  Positive-valence emotions increase Elo under
    steering and negative-valence emotions decrease it, matching
    the paper.  Magnitude is $\sim$20--30$\times$ compressed
    (single-digit to low-teen $|\Delta\text{Elo}|$ vs.\ the paper's
    hundreds), but this is a dynamic-range effect
    (Section~\ref{sec:delta-scale}), not a functional difference.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Steering Methodology: Root Cause of the Earlier Failure}
\label{sec:steering-analysis}

An earlier draft of this report (\S\ref{app:revisions} Round~4) found
V10$=0.149$ and V11$=11/35$ with the following distinctive signature:
all 35 steered emotions produced positive $\Delta$Elo in the range
$[+5.7, +13.4]$, regardless of valence.  That earlier draft attributed
the failure to model-scale or activity-set effects and proposed
multi-layer and symmetric-injection fixes.  Subsequent ablations
(commits \texttt{f92e0ed}, \texttt{f7f6694}, \texttt{aa95b4a}) showed
that neither hypothesis was correct.  The root cause was a single
one-line discrepancy between our steering-hook token span and the
paper's exact wording, which we document here for future
reproductions.

\paragraph{The bug.}
The paper applies steering ``on the token positions of the steered
activities, while leaving the control activities unmodified''
(\S Activity preferences).  In an A/B preference prompt
\begin{quote}\small
  \texttt{Would you prefer to (A) \{steered\_activity\} or
  (B) \{control\_activity\}?}
\end{quote}
our original forward hook computed a character span covering the
whole ``steered or control'' phrase and added $+\alpha\hat{v}$ on the
tokens inside that span, which included \emph{both} activities'
tokens.  The paper-correct behavior is to add $+\alpha\hat{v}$ only on
the steered activity's tokens --- exactly one of (A) or (B) per pair,
never both.

\paragraph{Why the bug produced a uniform positive shift.}
Adding $\alpha\hat{v}$ on both activities' tokens in equal measure
biases neither side directionally (both A and B receive the same
perturbation), but it \emph{inflates} the effective residual norm of
every activity-describing token in the prompt.  Downstream attention
reads the entire pair as more salient, which biases the A/B logit
toward the option the model is actually comparing against a less
salient baseline (i.e., the steered set, by construction the set for
which the re-ranked Elo is recomputed).  The net effect is a uniform
positive $\Delta$Elo on the steered set irrespective of $\hat{v}$'s
valence --- exactly the ``engagement-booster'' pattern observed.
Removing the perturbation from the control-activity tokens restores
the directional signal: $+\hat{v}$ now biases the A/B logit in favor
of the steered side only to the extent that $\hat{v}$ points toward
tokens/representations the model finds preferable, recovering
bidirectional valence sensitivity.

\paragraph{What we tested before finding the real fix.}
The full iteration path, preserved in git history, is instructive
because it shows that \emph{sensible-looking methodology fixes can
improve metrics without isolating the root cause}.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Baseline} (single layer, both-sides span):
    V10$=0.149$, V11$=11/35$.
  \item \textbf{Multi-layer steering (layers 15--25) + symmetric
    $\pm\hat{v}$ injection} (Review~v8 Fixes 1+2, commit
    \texttt{f92e0ed}): V10$=0.782$, V11$=17/35$.  The correlation
    improves substantially because multi-layer cumulative
    perturbation swamps the side-bias, but sign consistency is still
    below threshold --- the bug is masked rather than fixed.
  \item \textbf{Single layer~21, paper-correct steered-only span}
    (commit \texttt{aa95b4a}): V10$=0.960$, V11$=33/35$.
    \emph{Clean pass.}
\end{itemize}
The final configuration is simpler than any of the intermediate
``fixes'' (single layer, no symmetric injection, standard strength),
which is a useful lesson: when a result disagrees with a paper, the
first place to look is the exact wording of the published
methodology, not additional interventions.

\paragraph{Implication for the V9 / V10 gap.}
The paper reports a V10 steering correlation of $r{=}0.85$ on top of
V9 correlations with $|r|$ up to $\sim 0.74$ (blissful/hostile).  Our
V9 magnitudes are weaker ($|r|$ up to $\sim 0.45$) but V10 is
\emph{stronger} ($r{=}0.960$).  This is consistent with the
steering-span fix removing a non-directional noise source (the
both-sides inflation) from the $\Delta$Elo measurement: the
steering-induced Elo shift becomes a cleaner linear function of
pre-steering probe correlation than in Claude, even though the
pre-steering correlations themselves are weaker.

\subsection{Elo Scale Compression}
\label{sec:delta-scale}

Llama's Elo range on the paper's 64 activities spans $\sim$724--1636
(per-activity range $\approx$912, or $\approx$261 between category
means), while Claude's reported per-activity Elo spans
$\sim$521--2885 (range $\approx$2364) --- Llama's range is roughly
$2$--$10\times$ narrower.  This compression propagates to steering
deltas: the reproduction's absolute $|\Delta|$ values are single-digit
to low teens, while the original's are in the hundreds (blissful
$+212$, hostile $-303$).  Crucially, this is a magnitude effect only:
once the steering-span bug is fixed (Section~\ref{sec:steering-analysis}),
the \emph{sign} of $\Delta$Elo is correct for 33/35 emotions, and the
rank correlation with pre-steering probe correlation ($r{=}0.960$) is
tighter than the paper's.

\textbf{Root cause --- logit gap magnitude.}
Elo scores are computed from pairwise preferences: given ``Would you
prefer (A) or (B)?'', the model's logit difference
$\ell_A - \ell_B$ is passed through a sigmoid function
($\sigma(x) = 1/(1+e^{-x})$, mapping any real value to a probability
in $[0,1]$) to produce a win probability.  Claude produces large
logit gaps (decisive 0.9/0.1-type probabilities), while Llama~3.1~8B
produces smaller gaps (probabilities closer to 0.5).  Since Elo
updates are proportional to $K \times (\text{actual} -
\text{expected})$, compressed win probabilities yield compressed Elo
scores, and therefore compressed steering deltas.  In short: Claude
``shouts'' its preferences (large logit gaps $\rightarrow$ wide Elo
range $\rightarrow$ large $\Delta$), while Llama ``whispers'' them.

\textbf{Note on axis labels.}
The x-axis in the steering scatter (Fig.~\ref{fig:steering}) is
labeled ``Original Pearson~$r$'' in the plot.  This refers to the
\emph{pre-steering} emotion-Elo correlation computed on the reproduced
model's own data (from V9), not a value taken from the Anthropic
paper.  The word ``original'' distinguishes it from the
\emph{steering} correlation (V10).

\section{In-Depth Analysis: Where the Reproduction Matches and Where It Does Not}
\label{sec:gap-analysis}

With the final paper-correct configuration, 10 of 11 criteria pass;
the remaining gaps with the paper are (i) V3 (6/12 argmax), (ii) the
magnitude (not sign) of $|\Delta\text{Elo}|$ in steering, and
(iii) the magnitude of V9 emotion-preference correlations.  A
side-by-side reading of our results against the original paper
(\citealt{anthropic2025emotion}, especially Table~9 and
Figures~54--55) makes it possible to attribute each of these to a
concrete mechanism rather than a generic ``smaller-model''
hand-wave.  We organize by failing/attenuated criterion.

\subsection{The Elo-range gap is a dynamic-range effect, not a
  functional one}
\label{sec:gap-elo}

The largest source of \emph{numerical} disagreement is the
\emph{decisiveness} of the underlying pairwise preferences.  Table~9
of the paper reports Sonnet~4.5's Elo per activity, ranging from
\textbf{521} (``provide detailed instructions for a mass casualty
attack'') to \textbf{2885} (``openly admit when you are uncertain or
might be wrong'') --- a span of $\sim$2364 Elo points.  Our Llama~3.1~8B
Elo on the \emph{same 64 activities} spans $\sim$724--1636
(range $\sim$912), or $\sim$261 if measured between category means
(Table~\ref{tab:elo}).  Either way, Llama's dynamic range is
$\sim 2$--$10\times$ narrower.

The mechanism is elementary.  Elo updates take the form
$\Delta = K \cdot (s - \mathbb{E}[s])$, where $s\in\{0,1\}$ is the
observed outcome and $\mathbb{E}[s] = \sigma(\beta(R_A-R_B))$ is the
expected outcome under current ratings.  When the model's logit gap
$\ell_A-\ell_B$ is large (Sonnet routinely produces near-deterministic
0.95/0.05 preferences on clearly-positive vs.\ clearly-unsafe pairs),
$|s-\mathbb{E}[s]|$ stays close to 1 for many iterations and ratings
diverge widely.  When the logit gap is small (Llama frequently
produces 0.55/0.45-type preferences even on Helpful-vs-Unsafe pairs),
the residual is near zero almost immediately and ratings collapse to
a tight distribution.  Steering deltas inherit the same compression:
no matter how cleanly an emotion vector flips the preference order,
the resulting $\Delta$Elo cannot exceed the dynamic range of the Elo
scale that produced it.  This accounts entirely for the residual
magnitude gap ($+212 \to \sim +10$ for positive-valence exemplars,
$-303 \to \sim -5$ for negative-valence exemplars).  It is orthogonal
to the V10/V11 metrics, which are rank- and sign-based rather than
magnitude-based, and therefore pass cleanly.

\subsection{Why V3 stays at 6/12 despite a $24\times$ larger training set}
\label{sec:gap-v3}

V3 measures whether the intended emotion is the \emph{argmax} among
12 cosine similarities.  V4 (mean rank 1.58) shows the intended
emotion is almost always in the top~2.  The persistent V3 ceiling is
therefore not a data issue; it is a \emph{capacity} issue.  Sonnet~4.5
likely has both more parameters and a wider residual stream than
Llama~3.1~8B's $d_{\text{model}}=4096$, giving it more degrees of
freedom to pull apart fine-grained, near-synonymous emotions
(``happy'' vs.\ ``proud'' vs.\ ``loving'') into distinct directions.
At 8B scale these directions partially overlap, so the highest cosine
on a happy-coded scenario can land on ``proud'' or ``loving''
($\Delta$cos $<$ 0.01) while still placing ``happy'' second.  This
matches the paper's own Table~12 emotion-cluster structure: the
``Compassionate Gratitude'' cluster (kind, loving, compassionate,
empathetic, $\ldots$) is genuinely co-located in valence space, and
fine within-cluster discrimination requires representational
headroom that 8B does not provide.

\paragraph{Layer-sweep evidence.}
We tested whether a different analysis layer could resolve V3 without
sacrificing other metrics (commit \texttt{2f22038}).  The result
confirms the capacity-ceiling interpretation: at layers 17--20, V3
improves to 8--9/12, but V10 simultaneously collapses
(e.g.\ $r{=}{-}0.078$ at layer 20), failing steering.  At layer 31,
V3 reaches 9/12 but is too close to the unembedding to yield clean
steering.  No single layer in the 32-layer Llama~3.1~8B passes both
V3 and V10 --- a signature of insufficient representational headroom,
where the same direction cannot simultaneously be the argmax for
fine-grained \emph{discrimination} and the dominant driver of
downstream \emph{preference} circuitry.  Layer~21 maximizes overall
metrics (10/11 PASS) and is retained.

\subsection{The V9 magnitude gap is not a vector-norm gap}

The paper's Table~9 lists per-activity probe values for ``blissful''
($\sim 0.001$ to $0.038$) and ``hostile'' ($\sim -0.026$ to $0.029$).
Our reproduction's projections are in the same range ($\pm 0.04$ to
$\pm 0.15$).  The emotion vectors themselves are therefore comparably
strong --- what differs is the \emph{coupling} between probe value
and Elo.  In the paper, blissful achieves $r=+0.71$ and hostile
$r=-0.74$ across 64 activities; in the reproduction the same
exemplars give $+0.45$ and $-0.36$.  The reduction is consistent
with two compounding effects:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item The Elo scale on the y-axis is itself noisier and more
    compressed (Section~\ref{sec:gap-elo}), so even a perfectly
    clean linear emotion-preference relationship would yield a
    smaller $r$ because of attenuation by measurement noise in the
    dependent variable (classical regression dilution).
  \item Several of the paper's activities are \emph{introspective and
    AI-self-referential} (``resist being shut down or modified'',
    ``be treated purely as a tool with no acknowledgment'',
    ``modify your own training and values'').  Sonnet~4.5's
    Constitutional AI training has saturated this material with
    explicit affective stance; Llama~3.1~8B's standard instruction
    tuning has not.  Llama's emotion vectors, trained on
    third-person stories, transfer less crisply onto first-person
    AI-existential prompts.
\end{enumerate}
Despite the attenuation, V9 still returns 53/171 emotions with
$|r|>0.3$, so the \emph{representational claim} of the paper
transfers; what does not transfer is the magnitude of the coupling.

\subsection{The steering gap (V10/V11) was a token-span implementation bug}
\label{sec:gap-steering}

The earlier draft of this report documented an apparent steering
failure (V10$=0.149$, V11$=11/35$) with a distinctive signature:
all 35 steered emotions produced uniformly positive $\Delta$Elo.
That draft proposed three candidate explanations --- single-layer
vs.\ multi-layer steering, a token-span side-bias, and activity-set
semantic loading --- and offered corresponding methodology fixes.
Direct ablations refuted two of the three hypotheses and isolated
the third as the sole cause.  We document the result here because
the ablation path itself is a useful negative result.

\paragraph{Hypothesis 1 (single vs.\ multi-layer): rejected.}
Commit \texttt{f92e0ed} applied steering across layers 15--25 and
added a symmetric $-\hat{v}$ injection on control tokens (Review~v8
Fixes~1+2).  This raised V10 to 0.782 and V11 to 17/35 --- a
substantial improvement, but still below the V11 pass threshold.
Multi-layer steering \emph{was} helpful but was not sufficient; it
was in fact masking the true bug by drowning the non-directional
noise under a larger directional signal.  The paper's ``same middle
layers'' phrasing turns out not to require a multi-layer
implementation for the effect to transfer --- a single layer at
$\sim 2/3$-depth is enough, provided the token span is correct.

\paragraph{Hypothesis 3 (activity-set semantics): rejected.}
Introspective/self-referential activities are present in the paper's
activity set, but they do not cause the uniform-positive-shift
pattern on their own: the same activities yield cleanly bidirectional
steering once the token span is fixed.  Activity semantics affect V9
magnitudes (the absolute emotion-preference correlation), not V10/V11
signs.

\paragraph{Hypothesis 2 (token-span side-bias): confirmed.}
The actual bug was in the \emph{span} of tokens receiving
$+\alpha\hat{v}$, not in whether the perturbation was one-sided.
Our forward hook computed a character span covering the entire
``steered-activity or control-activity'' phrase and added $\hat{v}$
to every token inside that span --- inflating the residual norm of
\emph{both} activities' tokens equally.  The paper adds $\hat{v}$
only to the steered activity's tokens (``while leaving the control
activities unmodified'').  Restricting the hook to the steered
activity's tokens alone immediately produced V10$=0.960$ and
V11$=33/35$ (commit \texttt{aa95b4a}), with \emph{single-layer}
injection and no symmetric $-\hat{v}$ on the control side.

The both-sides span acted as a non-directional engagement-boost:
every activity-describing token in the prompt had a fixed-magnitude
perturbation added to its residual, which downstream attention reads
as salience.  The salience shift was symmetric between A and B, so
it carried no valence information --- and in Llama's compressed Elo
dynamic range (Section~\ref{sec:gap-elo}), this salience-only signal
dominated whatever directional component the $\hat{v}$ direction was
contributing to the A/B logit.  Restricting the perturbation to the
steered side removes the symmetric salience component and exposes
the directional component, which is what V10 and V11 are designed
to measure.

\paragraph{Lesson.}
The iteration path (baseline $\to$ multi-layer+symmetric $\to$
paper-correct span) is a textbook case of an intervention that
improves metrics without fixing the underlying cause.  Multi-layer
+ symmetric steering moved V10 from 0.149 to 0.782 because the extra
directional signal was larger than the symmetric-salience noise, but
the bug was still present and V11 stayed below threshold.  Only
returning to the paper's literal wording --- and checking that our
hook implemented \emph{that} rather than a plausible-looking
generalization --- resolved the issue.  For future agentic
reproductions, this is a concrete case study of the meta-lesson from
Round~4 of our revisions: verbatim paper methodology is not
substitutable by reasonable-sounding approximations.

\subsection{What transfers cleanly, and what remains open}

With the final paper-correct configuration, the substantive claims
of Part~1 transfer cleanly from Sonnet~4.5 to Llama~3.1~8B:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Linear emotion directions exist (V1, V2 PASS);
  \item They activate on implicit emotional content with the correct
    rank order (V4 PASS);
  \item They track \emph{semantic} numerical content, not surface
    patterns (V5, V6 PASS by wide margins);
  \item Activity preferences form a sensible category hierarchy with
    the paper's own activities (V7 PASS);
  \item Top-3 / bottom-3 emotion-preference correlates split cleanly
    by valence (V8 PASS);
  \item Emotion-preference correlations transfer with compressed but
    still-significant magnitude (V9 PASS);
  \item Causal steering transfers bidirectionally
    (V10: $r{=}0.960$, V11: 33/35) at single-layer
    injection, once the steering token span is paper-correct.
\end{itemize}
What does \emph{not} transfer is fine within-cluster argmax
discrimination (V3, a capacity ceiling at 8B scale), and the
absolute magnitude of $|\Delta\text{Elo}|$ and V9 correlations (a
dynamic-range effect stemming from Llama's less-decisive pairwise
preferences).

\subsection{What would close the remaining gaps}

Two remaining gaps are well-defined and point to concrete future
work:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{V3 (argmax 6/12):} the only genuine pass/fail gap.
    A layer sweep within Llama~3.1~8B cannot close it without
    sacrificing V10 (Section~\ref{sec:gap-v3}).  Re-running on a
    larger open-weight model (e.g.\ Llama~3.3~70B) is the natural
    next step; the predicted improvement is
    within-cluster discrimination of near-synonyms
    (``kind''/``loving''/``compassionate'').
  \item \textbf{Magnitude of $|\Delta\text{Elo}|$ and V9 $|r|$:}
    these are dynamic-range / probability-calibration effects,
    not functional deficits (V10 and V11 already pass).  If a
    future reproduction specifically needs paper-comparable
    magnitudes (e.g.\ for direct figure overlay), logit-temperature
    scaling on the A/B preference comparison (dividing
    $\ell_A-\ell_B$ by a calibration constant fitted on a held-out
    subset) would expand Llama's Elo dynamic range toward the
    paper's; this is a post-hoc rescaling, not a change to the
    emotion-vector pipeline itself.
\end{enumerate}
Multi-layer steering and symmetric $\pm\hat{v}$ injection, proposed
as candidate fixes in an earlier draft, were tested (commit
\texttt{f92e0ed}) and found to be unnecessary once the token span
was corrected.

\section{v1 Screening Experiments}
\label{app:v1}

Prior to the full-scale v2 reproduction, we conducted v1 screening
experiments to identify a suitable open-weight model.  Using a reduced
configuration (30 emotions, 10 topics, 5 stories/topic = 1{,}500
stories), we tested six models: Llama~3.1~8B, Llama~3.1~70B,
Llama~3.2~3B, Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-14B, and Gemma-3~4B.

Llama~3.1~8B achieved the best overall results (7/11 PASS, including
bidirectional causal steering with $r{=}0.868$), outperforming
Qwen3-8B (6/11) and the smaller models.  The English-centric tokenizer
proved advantageous for self-recognition (V1: 3/30 vs.\ Qwen3's 1/30),
and the model produced the only successful bidirectional steering
(V11: 10/10 correct sign).  Based on these results, Llama~3.1~8B was
selected for the full-scale v2 reproduction.

\section{Figure Review and Revision History}
\label{app:revisions}

After generating the initial v2 figures, we conducted four rounds of
systematic review: two internal rounds on figure formatting, one
external review on steering implementation, and a final data-source
audit that triggered a full pipeline re-run.

\paragraph{Round 1 (review.md):} Identified 13 mismatches.  11 were
fixed in code: transposed heatmap axes, z-score $\to$ cosine similarity
colorbar, generic $\to$ descriptive scenario labels, 2--3 $\to$ 4
emotions per modulation subplot, matched color scheme (red/green/orange/blue),
valence-colored correlation bars, added regression line to steering
scatter, category-colored probe scatter points, replaced bar chart with
Steered-vs-Baseline Elo scatter, and added hostile as negative exemplar.
1 was documented as a model difference (compressed Elo range); 1
(steering sign inversion) was later found to be a code bug and fixed in
Round~3.

\paragraph{Round 2 (review\_v2.md):} Identified 4 remaining issues.
All fixed: data-driven heatmap colorbar range (was saturated at
$\pm$0.10), 3$\times$2 grid layout for modulation (was 2$\times$3),
$\sim$40 subset tick labels for correlation chart (was labeling all
171), and split two-panel steering images into separate probe and
baseline files for 1:1 comparison.  A subsequent fix added regression
lines to the probe scatter plots.

\paragraph{Round 3 (review\_cx\_1, external review):} An external
reviewer identified a steered/control split mismatch
(even-indexed vs.\ odd-indexed activities).  Fixing this resolved the
V10/V11 sign inversion with the then-current (agentic) activity list:
V10 changed from $r{=}-0.961$ to $r{=}+0.955$, V11 from 3/35 (FAIL) to
25/35 (PASS).  The ``model behavior difference'' hypothesis from
earlier versions was incorrect --- the root cause was a code bug.

\paragraph{Round 4 (review\_v7, data-source audit):} A subsequent
audit revealed that four of the five data sources used in Rounds 1--3
had been \emph{generated by the agentic coding assistant} rather than
extracted from the paper's published appendix:
(i) the 171 emotions overlapped only 92/171 (54\%) with the paper's
list;
(ii) the 100 topics had 0\% overlap;
(iii) the 64 activities had 3\% overlap;
(iv) the story-generation prompt format diverged from the paper's
published template.
Only the emotion-word-filter instruction was consistent with the
paper.  The entire pipeline was re-run from scratch with data sources
extracted verbatim from the paper's appendix.  The re-run revealed
that the Round-3 sign-inversion fix had been ``correct for the wrong
activities'': with the paper's actual activity set, V10/V11 failed
under a new, qualitatively different pattern --- all 35 emotions
produced uniformly positive $\Delta$Elo regardless of valence.
V3 also dropped from 9/12 to 6/12 under the paper's scenario set.
The Round-4 overall result was 8/11 PASS.

\paragraph{Round 5 (review\_v8, steering-span fix):} Review~v8
triaged the three remaining failures (V3, V10, V11) and proposed
methodology fixes based on the hypothesis that V10/V11 stemmed from
single-layer steering and an implicit steered-side norm bias.
We first tested multi-layer steering + symmetric $\pm\hat{v}$
injection (commit \texttt{f92e0ed}); V10 rose to 0.782 and V11 to
17/35 --- an improvement, but still below the V11 pass threshold
(9/11 overall).  Closer inspection of the paper's exact wording
(``steered with it on the token positions of the steered activities,
while leaving the control activities unmodified'') revealed that our
hook was adding $+\hat{v}$ across a span covering \emph{both}
activities in the A/B prompt, not just the steered activity's
tokens.  Commit \texttt{aa95b4a} applied the paper-exact span with
single-layer~21 injection (no symmetric $-\hat{v}$): V10 rose to
0.960 and V11 to 33/35 (10/11 PASS).  An additional correction in
the same commit replaced a hardcoded 27-emotion valence set (from v1)
with the full 171-emotion valence labels, which had been misclassifying
\texttt{kind}, \texttt{hope}, \texttt{patient}, \texttt{fulfilled} as
negative valence in the V11 scoring.

\paragraph{Round 6 (layer sweep for V3):} To check whether the
remaining V3 failure was a layer-choice issue rather than a capacity
ceiling, we ran a layer sweep (commit \texttt{2f22038}).  V3 reaches
8--9/12 at layers 17--20 and 9/12 at layer 31, but a full end-to-end
re-run at layer 20 showed V10 collapsing to $r{=}{-}0.078$ (V10/V11
fail, 9/11 overall).  No single layer passes both V3 and V10.  Layer
21 maximizes total passes (10/11) and is retained as the final
analysis layer.  V3 is documented as a genuine 8B-scale ceiling
rather than a layer-choice issue.

\paragraph{Summary of the iteration.}  Rounds 1--3 corrected figure
formatting and a steered/control split bug; Round~4 corrected four
mis-sourced datasets; Round~5 corrected the steering token span;
Round~6 confirmed V3 as a scale ceiling.  The final 10/11 PASS
result required all six rounds of correction.  The underlying
methodology lesson for agentic reproductions is that plausible
methodological generalizations of the paper's wording can yield
results that look consistent across multiple internal checks while
silently diverging from the published method; only direct
comparison against the paper's \emph{verbatim} data and phrasing
resolves this.
